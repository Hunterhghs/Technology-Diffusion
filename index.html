Theoretical Foundations

How Technology Spreads

Decades of research have produced frameworks that explain why some technologies diffuse rapidly while others stall — and why geography, institutions, and timing matter as much as the technology itself.

Diffusion of Innovations

Everett Rogers, 1962

Adoption follows an S-curve across five adopter categories: innovators, early adopters, early majority, late majority, and laggards. Relative advantage, compatibility, complexity, trialability, and observability determine adoption speed. In emerging economies, the early-adopter phase is often compressed when technologies arrive pre-validated from advanced markets.

Technological Leapfrogging

Various, 1990s–Present

Latecomers bypass intermediate stages of development entirely. Sub-Saharan Africa skipped fixed-line telephony for mobile; Kenya built a mobile payments ecosystem before most of its population had bank accounts. Leapfrogging is most pronounced when legacy infrastructure is absent and switching costs are low.

Advantage of Backwardness

Alexander Gerschenkron, 1962

Less-developed economies can grow faster by importing and adapting proven technologies rather than inventing from scratch. The larger the gap with the frontier, the greater the potential catch-up rate — provided institutions, human capital, and absorptive capacity are sufficient to exploit imported knowledge.

Absorptive Capacity

Cohen & Levinthal, 1990

A nation's ability to recognize, assimilate, and apply external knowledge depends on its existing knowledge base. R&D investment, educational attainment, and institutional quality determine whether imported technology becomes productive or remains unused. India's 11-year reinvention lag vs. the U.S.'s 3 years illustrates this gap.

Regional Analysis

Divergent Paths, Converging Ambitions

Emerging economies are not a monolith. Each region brings unique strengths, constraints, and adoption trajectories shaped by demographics, governance, and infrastructure.

Sub-Saharan Africa

Leapfrog Innovation Hub

The region that best illustrates technological leapfrogging. With only 36% internet penetration, Sub-Saharan Africa might appear a laggard — but it leads the world in mobile money adoption, with M-Pesa and its successors processing billions in transactions across a population that largely bypassed traditional banking.

Renewable energy capacity doubled in 2025, reaching 12 GW of new installations. South Africa alone installed over 3 GW of solar PV for the first time. Rwanda's national drone delivery network for medical supplies has become a global model for last-mile logistics in infrastructure-constrained environments.

The primary constraint remains connectivity: only 12% of Africans have access to 5G networks (vs. 74% in Europe), and Middle Africa's internet penetration sits at approximately 25%. Yet mobile phone access reaches 65–70% of the population, providing a platform for the next wave of digital services.

South Asia

Scale-Driven Digital Transformation

India dominates the region's technology narrative. UPI processed 228.3 billion transactions in 2025, making it the world's largest real-time payment system. Internet penetration surged to 70%, up from under 40% a decade ago. India's annual renewable capacity additions grew by nearly 60% in 2025, with almost 50 GW of solar PV commissioned.

AI adoption is accelerating, with 35% adoption rates and India ranking among the top generators of ChatGPT traffic globally. The country's AADHAAR biometric identity system, covering 1.3 billion residents, provides digital infrastructure for financial inclusion, welfare distribution, and e-governance at a scale no other country has achieved.

Yet South Asia still has the largest absolute number of unconnected people — roughly 747 million as of late 2025. Pakistan is an emerging bright spot, installing around 10 GW of solar PV in 2025, while Bangladesh's garment-sector digitization and Sri Lanka's IT services exports show varied pathways to technology adoption.

Southeast Asia

The Super-App Ecosystem

Southeast Asia's technology diffusion is defined by the super-app model — platforms like Grab, GoTo, and Sea Group that bundle payments, e-commerce, ride-hailing, and financial services into a single mobile interface. This bundling strategy drives rapid adoption by reducing friction for first-time digital users.

AI adoption is surging. Between 2024 and 2025, nearly 130,000 companies in the Philippines and Vietnam began using AI tools, with adoption rates jumping 50% in the Philippines and 39% in Vietnam. Thailand's AI usage grew 36.2% in early 2026, among the fastest rates globally.

The region's young, mobile-first population and rapidly growing middle class create favorable conditions for technology adoption. Indonesia's digital economy alone is projected to be the largest in ASEAN, with digital payments expanding at a 34.24% CAGR across Asia-Pacific, driven in part by the region's super-app ecosystems.

Latin America

Fintech Revolution & Digital Government

Latin America's technology diffusion story centers on fintech and digital financial inclusion. Brazil's Pix instant payment system, launched in 2020, now processes more transactions per capita than India's UPI, transforming a country where 45 million adults were previously unbanked. Mexico's AI adoption rate has reached 38%, the highest in the region.

Brazil leads as one of the world's largest generators of ChatGPT traffic from middle-income countries, reflecting broad consumer AI adoption. Chile and Uruguay have emerged as regional leaders in renewable energy, with Chile's solar capacity in the Atacama Desert making it competitive with global leaders on a per-capita basis.

Challenges remain in infrastructure equity: rural broadband access lags significantly behind urban centers, and the region's relatively high smartphone costs (as a share of income) create barriers to participation in the digital economy. Regulatory fragmentation across countries complicates cross-border digital service expansion.

Middle East & North Africa

State-Led Digitization & Energy Transition

The MENA region presents a bifurcated technology landscape. Gulf states — particularly the UAE, Saudi Arabia, and Qatar — pursue top-down digital transformation backed by sovereign wealth, investing heavily in AI infrastructure, smart cities (NEOM, Masdar), and advanced logistics. The UAE ranks among the highest in the world for government digitization.

North African economies like Egypt and Morocco are emerging as near-shore tech hubs for European companies, leveraging geographic proximity, competitive labor costs, and improving digital infrastructure. Egypt's fintech sector has grown rapidly, while Morocco is becoming a renewable energy leader with its Noor-Ouarzazate solar complex.

The region's technology diffusion is heavily influenced by state policy rather than organic market forces. While this enables rapid deployment of infrastructure, it can limit grassroots innovation and startup ecosystems outside of designated economic zones.

Case Studies

Breakthroughs That Redefined the Possible

These cases demonstrate how emerging economies have not merely adopted technology, but reimagined it to serve populations and contexts that advanced-economy solutions were never designed for.

🇰🇪

M-Pesa

Kenya → 7 countries • Launched 2007

Safaricom's M-Pesa demonstrated that financial inclusion doesn't require bank branches. Using simple SMS on feature phones, it allowed unbanked Kenyans to send, receive, and store money. It now has over 90% penetration in Kenya and has expanded across Africa and into Asia, processing billions of dollars annually and spawning an ecosystem of services from micro-insurance to agricultural credit.

Unified Payments Interface

India • Launched 2016

Built as public digital infrastructure by India's National Payments Corporation, UPI is an open, interoperable platform enabling instant bank-to-bank transfers via mobile phones with zero fees for individuals. It processed 228.3 billion transactions in 2025, now accounts for 84% of all electronic transactions in India, and has over 500 million unique users. UPI accounts for 55% of all e-commerce payments and represents the world's most successful public digital payments infrastructure.

Zipline Drone Delivery

Rwanda → Global • Launched 2016

Rwanda became the first country to deploy a national drone delivery network for medical supplies. Zipline's autonomous aircraft deliver blood, vaccines, and medications to remote health centers in under 30 minutes — a journey that previously took hours by road. The system has since expanded to Ghana, Nigeria, Kenya, and the U.S., demonstrating that frontier technology can be deployed first in emerging markets where the need is greatest.

China's Solar Manufacturing

China • 2000s–Present

China transformed from a solar technology importer to the dominant global manufacturer, now producing over 80% of the world's solar panels. This scale-driven cost reduction — solar module prices fell over 90% in 15 years — made solar energy economically viable for developing countries worldwide. In 2025, solar PV accounted for more than three-quarters of all new global renewable capacity, a trajectory enabled largely by China's industrial policy and manufacturing scale.

Barriers & Challenges

What Slows Technology Diffusion

Understanding the structural constraints that impede adoption is essential for designing effective policy interventions and investment strategies.

Infrastructure Deficit

Unreliable electricity, limited broadband backbone, and sparse last-mile connectivity remain fundamental constraints. Without stable power and basic connectivity, even proven technologies cannot function.

Human Capital Gaps

Technical skills shortages, low digital literacy, and brain drain to advanced economies limit absorptive capacity. Without skilled workers to implement, maintain, and adapt technologies, hardware investments underperform.

Green Technology Transfer

The Renewable Energy Surge

Falling costs — driven largely by China's manufacturing scale — have made solar and wind economically competitive in emerging markets, triggering an unprecedented wave of clean energy deployment.

2025: A Record Year for Emerging Markets

Global renewable capacity additions reached 800 GW in 2025, a 16% increase. Solar PV accounted for more than three-quarters of all additions. Critically, the growth story is no longer limited to China: India's renewable additions grew by nearly 60%, with almost 50 GW of solar PV commissioned. Sub-Saharan Africa doubled its renewable capacity, reaching 12 GW of new installations.

Pakistan installed approximately 10 GW of solar PV, emerging as a significant market. Wind additions are accelerating across Africa, the Middle East, ASEAN, Latin America, and Eurasia as policy frameworks improve and costs continue to decline.

This transformation demonstrates a key dynamic of technology diffusion in emerging economies: once enabling conditions align — cost competitiveness, policy support, and proven technology — adoption can accelerate rapidly, often outpacing projections from just a few years prior.

Future Outlook

Artificial Intelligence & the Next Frontier

AI represents both the greatest opportunity and the greatest risk for emerging economies — a technology that could accelerate convergence or deepen the divide.

The Promise

AI adoption is spreading faster than any previous general-purpose technology, with middle-income countries already generating over 40% of global GenAI traffic.
  • Rapid uptake: AI adoption reached 38% in Mexico, 35% in India, and 28% in Indonesia. Asia accounts for 10 of the world's 15 fastest-growing AI markets.
  • Enterprise expansion: 130,000 companies in the Philippines and Vietnam began using AI between 2024–2025. South Korea's usage surged 43.2%.
  • Sector impact: Precision agriculture, digital health diagnostics, climate modeling, and automated customer service are high-impact applications for emerging markets.
  • Language access: Multilingual LLMs are democratizing access to information and services in local languages for the first time.

The Peril

Despite rapid adoption, the AI divide is widening. Infrastructure, data, and compute constraints threaten to concentrate AI's benefits in already-advanced economies.
  • Widening gap: AI adoption in the Global North grew nearly 2x faster than in the Global South. 24.7% of working-age population in the North uses AI tools vs. 14.1% in the South.
  • Compute concentration: Training frontier AI models requires infrastructure that only a handful of countries possess. This creates structural dependency on external providers.
  • Data sovereignty: Much of the training data for commercial AI is generated in and optimized for advanced-economy contexts, potentially limiting relevance for local needs.
  • Labor disruption: AI-driven automation may reduce the cost advantage of emerging-market labor, potentially undermining the traditional development pathway of export-led manufacturing.
